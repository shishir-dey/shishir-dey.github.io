Einstein: Oh, I’m sorry. I must have the wrong room.

Mileva: What class are you looking for?

Einstein: Physics.

Mileva: Good. Tell me, do you know Empedocles?

Einstein: Excuse me?

Mileva: Empedocles. The pre-Socratic philosopher who studied cosmogony.

Einstein: As I said, I’m in the physics class.

Mileva: Yes, you made that quite clear. Cosmogony is the study of the origins of the cosmos, and Empedocles was the first known person to ask the simple question: “What is light?”

Einstein: I’m sorry, who are you?

Mileva: He believed that Aphrodite, a woman, in case you didn’t know, formed the human eye and lit a fire in it, allowing us to see.

Einstein: Well, that’s ridiculous. If that were the case, then we could see equally well at night.

Mileva: Empedocles thought the same. So he postulated that the sun’s rays might be linked to the rays shooting from our eyes. A fanciful idea, I know, but if not for him, you might not have had the counter-theories of Aristotle, then Huygens, Kepler, Newton, Faraday, and finally James Clerk Maxwell: “Light is electromagnetic radiation.”

Einstein: I apologize. I didn’t think...

Mileva: ...that a woman could possibly understand that the curl of the electric field is equal to the negative time derivative of the magnetic field?

Einstein: ...Faraday’s law.

Mileva: The Maxwell–Faraday equation, if we’re being specific.

Mileva: You see, Empedocles was wrong about how we see, but he stumbled upon a universal truth: human perception is frightfully narrow. We believe we see the whole when, in fact, we’ve only seen a fraction.

- Genius S1.E2 (TV Series)
